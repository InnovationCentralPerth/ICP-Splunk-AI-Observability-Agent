This is the ICP Smart Building Observability Platform — a live AI observability system built by Innovation Central Perth at Curtin University, running on real Cisco Meraki hardware right here in our space.

Every two minutes the platform polls three data sources simultaneously: our MT10 environmental sensor for temperature and humidity, our MS355 network switch for per-port traffic and power consumption, and three MV12W cameras with Meraki MV Sense occupancy analytics — covering the ICP Workshop, the Demo Area, and the Pantry.

All of this streams into Splunk Enterprise via HTTP Event Collector, where Splunk's Machine Learning Toolkit runs anomaly detection and time-series forecasting on the ingested data.

But we have added a second AI layer that runs entirely inside the agent. Every ten minutes, the Camera AI module fetches a full twenty-four hours of per-minute occupancy data from each camera — that is over fourteen hundred data points per camera — and runs crowd-surge detection in-process, with no external ML dependency.

Click any of the camera buttons in the sidebar and you get a full twenty-four hour occupancy timeline. The blue bars show entrances per minute. The green line tracks average occupancy. And the amber diamond markers — those are live Machine Learning anomaly flags plotted directly on the chart. Hover over a marker and you see exactly what triggered it: the anomaly type, severity, and a plain-English description.

The anomaly detection runs on three distinct conditions. First, crowd surge: a sudden entrance spike that is three times or more the typical rate for that hour — this catches unexpected gatherings rather than just absolute headcounts. Second, equipment thermal events: when temperature or humidity spikes but the cameras show zero occupancy — meaning the heat source is not human. That correlation helps us distinguish people warming a room from hardware that might be overheating. Third, threshold triggers: overcrowding above five persons raises a medium alert, and any presence detected between six in the evening and seven in the morning raises a high alert.

When any anomaly is confirmed, the Automated Response Engine fires. It generates an LLM-written incident report, creates a persistent saved alert in Splunk via the REST API, and logs the response as a new event back into Splunk. Data flows in, Splunk AI detects, the LLM interprets, and the automated response flows back out — a complete closed loop.

The natural language chat interface is grounded in all of this live data. Ask a question and the LLM pulls in the current sensor readings, the active anomalies, the camera AI findings, and the Splunk results before answering. Every response explicitly cites what Splunk detected.

Built on real hardware, running live, open source on GitHub.
